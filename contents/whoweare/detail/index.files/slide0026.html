Tobacco History in Japan (1)
Introduction of Tobacco to Japan
While documentation that would give an exact date as to when tobacco was introduced in Japan is not available, it is believed
that tobacco was originally brought by the Portuguese in the early sixteenth century and was cultivated in Japan at the end of
the sixteenth or early seventeenth century. Furthermore, contemporary paintings of that period vividly illustrate the use of
tobacco by Japanese in their everyday life.
The oldest document that mentions the use of tobacco products and tobacco seeds is a Spanish report dating from 1601. The
Burguillos Report refers to an audience granted by Tokugawa Ieyasu Shogun to the Spanish Franciscan missionary Jeronimo
de Jesus, who wished to discuss the possibility of trade between Spain and Japan.
In 1609, the younger brother of Emperor Goyozei, Prince Toshihito, wrote about tobacco as Åga new herb from across the sea".
In addition, he revealed the extent of tobacco use among the populace.
The evolution of smoking in Japan was very unique and could not be found in any other country. Smoking quickly become
localized and ritualistic. Smokers vied with one another in displaying their smoking paraphernalia when they socialized. As the
pipes evolved in the Edo Period (1600-1868), they became shorter and pipe holders were designed so that the pipes in the
holders could be attached to the obi or sash of the kimono.
Regulations Prohibiting the use of Tobacco
In 1609 an event occurred that forced the Shogun to issue an ordinance forbidding the use of tobacco. The reason was not because tobacco had
become a medical risk. It had, indirectly, become a physical risk. In the ancient capital of Kyoto, the Leather Breaches and the Bramble smoking
clubs had been established. These smoking clubs attracted young men from the upper strata of society. These young men carried extremely long
and heavy smoking pipes. These young men went about provoking fights in public. It did not take long before the law-abiding segment of society
loudly protested this decimation of the ÅgwaÅh and the clubs were permanently closed. As an after-affect, smoking was prohibited. No one took the
prohibition edict to heart and smoking continued. Further edicts were issued, each time more severe, and each with no effect. In 1624 the laws
prohibiting smoking were repealed. From that time onward the smoking of tobacco was enjoyed as a luxury commodity throughout polite
Japanese society and by both sexes.
The custom of using finely-shred tobacco was a significant proof in the history of Japanese technology for the original hand-shredding proocess was
soon supplemented by a variety of mechanical techniques, that stimulated the early development of quality mass-production machinery in Japan.
