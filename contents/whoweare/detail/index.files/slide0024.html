Tobacco History in Japan (2)
Meiji Era: Tobacco Taxation
After the Meiji restoration of 1868, the government of Japan set about reorganizing the country as a modern industrial state. One of the first major
problems awaiting settlement was the critical question of government finances. Since tobacco and tobacco products had long been subject to
various duties at provincial and local levels, the Meiji administrators were quick to implement a system of national tobacco taxation.
With the Japanese authorities constrained by treaty to levy a nominal import tariff of 5% on the trade, the American- manufactured cigarettes were
sufficiently cheap to compete with the locally produced pipe tobacco and mouthpiece cigarettes made from domestically grown leaf. After the treaty
revisions were announced the Meiji government raised the duty on tobacco imports to 40% from January 1899.
The Meiji government was well aware of the potential that tobacco presented for the purposes of revenue generation and in 1898 it had created
a state monopoly for the purchase of all domestically gown leaf upon which it had then imposed an excise tax at the rate of 100 % ad valorem. For a
time this benefited importers of foreign leaf tobacco, and led one Japanese tobacco manufacturer, the Murai Brothers Tobacco Company of Kyoto,
to substitute imported American leaf for domestically grown tobacco in its products. However this avenue was closed in August 1899 when the
state monopoly was extended to cover imported tobacco leaf as well. At the same time the import tariff on tobacco goods was raised from 40% to 100%.
This decision effectively torpedoed the trade in imported manufactured cigarettes and left the Western firms to consider their response.
